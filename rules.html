RULES YOU MUST KNOW BEFORE PLAYING A TOURNAMENT

TOUCH-MOVE RULE

The touch-move rule in chess specifies that a player, having the move, who
deliberately touches a piece on the board must move or capture that piece if it is
legal to do so. If it is the player's piece that was touched,
it must be moved if the piece has a legal move.


  • A judge of "deliberacy" should be completed by an arbiter.
  • If a player wishes to adjust a piece, they must verbally announce so.
  • Adjustments can be announced with the English "adjust" or the
    French "j'adoube"

    • GM Garry Kasparov touches a piece vs. GM Judit Polgar and
    takes back his move after realizing it was a blunder. He was widely critiqued
    for violating the touch-move rule.

    CLOCKS

    At the beginning of the game, the player with black begins the clock.
    The players push the clock after each move, alternating in succession until the game ends.
    The clock must only be paused to call over an arbiter.


  • The clock is to be set before the game by an arbiter.
  • If a player runs out of time, their opponent is responsible for calling them out!

    • Even the world's best fall under time trouble.
    World Champion Ding Liren blunders the advantage in
    a game vs. GM Ian Nepomniachtchi for the title.

    SPORTSMANSHIP

    It is impertinent that both players shake hands before and after the game.
    Talking should be frowned upon during games unless completely necessary.

  • Shake hands before and after the game, regardless of result.
  • To offer a draw or resign, verbally notify your opponent of your intention.

    • Sportsmanship is important, even at the highest levels.
    GM Magnus Carlsen offers a handshake to his opponent, GM Hikaru Nakamura.
